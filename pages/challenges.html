Ethics of AI in Healthcare Research and Technology

Landscape Challenges

Ethical Challenges and Case Studies in AI Healthcare

Here, we examine real-world cases and their ethical implications for biomedical engineering.

Case Study 1: IBM Watson for Oncology

In 2011, IBM's Watson won Jeapordy! Hoping to extend this model, IBM decided to focus on tackling healthcare challenges. They released Watson for Oncology, which aimed to analyze vast amounts of medical literature and data about cancer diagnoses and treatments, in order to recommend treatment plans to oncologists. However, the ambitious vision of an evidence-based assistant to oncologists soon led to several problems.

The Problem

Watson was trained heavily using data from hospitals like Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center (MSKCC). However, initial trials in India and China showed that the model's recommendations were inconsistent with local physicians, as the model displayed a US-centric focus on treatment plans and drug choices. Criticism continued to increase, and investigative reports found that some of Watson’s recommendations were based on hypothetical scenarios rather than real-world data. Eventually, IBM shut down the program, and later postmortem analyses found that systemic issues like poor data quality, inadequate clinical validation, and unrealistic timelines had plagued the model.

Ethical Principles

Transparency

  • The "Black box" decision-making process made it harder to interpret how the model was making its conclusions.
  • There were limited accountability measures to allow the model to be more flexible.

Healthcare Equity

  • The model's training was biased to prefer a single institution's diagonstic and treatment style.
  • Resource availability issues: no consideration for variations in things like access to drugs or technology across different parts of the globe made the model not adaptable enough.

Engineering Implications

Data Diversity

Engineers working on such ML/AI-systems should ensure they use diverse, real-world patient data with proper validation. Synthetic data should be used with utmost caution.

System Design

Engineers should design complex systems such as Watson to be more explainable- things like reasoning chains and confidence metrics that justify why a model made a certain choice should be easily accesible at all levels.

Engineers also need to gather enough feedback from everyone involved, form physicians to patients to pharmatcists, to ensure all these perspectives are accounted for in development.

Marketing and Rollouts

With technologies like this, the development should be iterative in nature- instead of just releasing a model, smarter deployment techniques like phased rollouts should be utilized.

Additionally, companies must be cautious in their marketing claims- IBM's aggressive marketing severely exaggerated Watson's capabilities, and didn't focus on its limitations, creating unrealistic expectations.

Case Study 2 : Babylon Health's AI Symptom Checker

Babylon Health was a UK tech start-up that rose to being worth over billions of dollars and then quickly collapsed. Babylon's "revolutionary" product was a symptom-checking chatbot. The bot would assess symptoms and perform triage, claiming that this would help doctors make diagnoses and cut waiting lists. This promise of being the "Google of healthcare" gained popularity from several investors and politicians, leading the company to raise close to a billion dollars in less than 10 years.

The Problem

The chatbot failed to live up to the grand expectations. Physicians pointed out how it failed to catch symptoms of a heart attack, would make severely off diagnoses, with an investigation concluding the bot performs "significantly worse" than a doctor. A former employee later disclosed the "algorithm" was nothing more than a tree of if-else statements, and didn't exactly employ ML/AI techniques. Ultimately, the company lost 99% of its value within 18 months, and was later sold off.

Ethical Principles

Safety vs. Innovation

  • Fast Development process: the company embraced the "move fast, break stuff" mindset typical of tech startups. However, given the sensitive nature of this product, it was much more important to add several safety guardrails and appropriate methods of validating the chatbot responses instead of trying to move fast.
  • Lack of diverse perspectives: the company didn't really work with doctors and other medical professionals actively throughout development, leading to several inconsistencies being observed upon release.

Integrity

  • The company was dishonest in their advertising, claiming the decisions were powered with "artificial intelligence", while in reality it was an Excel decision tree.
  • The Dishonest claims were also dangerous as they were propagated by influential people like investors and politicians, causing more and more patients to mistakenly put their trust in the product.

Engineering Implications

Honest Communication

When building these complex systems, engineers should never simplify their explanations at the sake of misrepresenting what it does. Over-simplifying the explanations can lead to misleaden perceptions, and patients might not realize the limitations of these technologies. Engineers should also be honest when communicating with stakeholders like investors and politicians, as false advertising may be propagated through them to the general public and have much wider-reaching consequences.

Testing Requirements

Companies working in this space should have strict government-regulated testing, such as third-parties testing the models, as well as internal teams dedicated to "breaking" these models. The company should be logging all the safety metrics and presenting them publicly.
